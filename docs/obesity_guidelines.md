There are many reasons why some people have trouble losing weight. Often, obesity results from inherited, physiological, and environmental factors combined with diet, physical activity, and exercise choices. 
The good news is that even modest weight loss can improve or prevent the health conditions associated with obesity. A healthier diet, increased physical activity, and behavior changes can help you lose weight. Prescription medicines and weight-loss procedures are other options for treating obesity. 

## Symptoms

Body Mass Index (BMI) is often used to diagnose obesity. To calculate BMI: 
- Multiply weight in pounds by 703, divide by height in inches, and then divide again by height in inches. 
- Or divide weight in kilograms by height in meters squared. 

### BMI Categories 

| BMI | Weight Status |
| --- | --- |
| Below 18.5 | Underweight |
| 18.5 – 24.9 | Healthy |
| 25.0 – 29.9 | Overweight |
| 30.0 and higher | Obesity |

Note: Asians with a BMI of 23 or higher may have an increased risk of health problems. 

For most people, BMI provides a reasonable estimate of body fat. However, BMI does not directly measure body fat. Some individuals, such as muscular athletes, may fall into the obesity category despite having low body fat. 

Many healthcare professionals also measure waist circumference to guide treatment decisions: 
- Men: Greater than 40 inches (102 cm) 
- Women: Greater than 35 inches (89 cm) 

Body fat percentage may also be used during a weight-loss program to track progress. 

### When to See a Doctor 

If you're concerned about your weight or weight-related health conditions, consult a healthcare professional about obesity management. Together, you can evaluate your health risks and discuss weight-loss options. 

## Causes

Although genetic, behavioral, metabolic, and hormonal factors influence body weight, obesity occurs when you consume more calories than you burn through daily activities and exercise. Your body stores these excess calories as fat. 
In many cases, diets are high in calories due to fast food and high-calorie beverages. 

People with obesity may: 
- Eat more calories before feeling full 
- Feel hungry more often 
- Eat more due to stress or anxiety 

Modern lifestyles often involve less physical activity, resulting in fewer calories burned throughout the day. 

## Risk Factors 

Obesity often results from a combination of causes and contributing factors. 

### Family Inheritance and Influences 

Genes inherited from parents may affect: 
- Amount of body fat stored 
- Distribution of body fat 
- Appetite regulation 
- Energy conversion 
- Calorie burning during exercise 

Obesity also tends to run in families because eating and activity habits are often shared. 

### Lifestyle Choices 

Unhealthy Diet: A diet high in calories, low in fruits and vegetables, rich in fast food, and filled with sugary beverages and oversized portions contributes to weight gain. 
Liquid Calories: Calories consumed through beverages may not create a feeling of fullness. Alcohol and sugary drinks can significantly increase calorie intake. 
Inactivity: An inactive lifestyle makes it easy to consume more calories than are burned. Excessive screen time is strongly associated with weight gain. 

### Certain Diseases and Medicines 

Medical conditions linked to obesity include: 
- Hypothyroidism 
- Cushing syndrome 
- Prader-Willi syndrome 
- Arthritis and other mobility-limiting conditions 

Medications that may contribute to weight gain include: 
- Steroids 
- Some antidepressants 
- Antiseizure medications 
- Diabetes medications 
- Antipsychotic medications 
- Certain beta blockers 

### Social and Economic Issues 

Limited access to safe places for exercise, healthy foods, and nutrition education can increase obesity risk. Social circles may also influence eating and activity habits. 

### Age 

Obesity can occur at any age. As people age: 
- Hormonal changes occur 
- Physical activity often decreases 
- Muscle mass tends to decline 
- Metabolism slows down 

These changes make weight gain more likely. 

### Other Factors 

Pregnancy: Weight gained during pregnancy can sometimes be difficult to lose and may contribute to obesity later. 
Quitting Smoking: Smoking cessation is often associated with weight gain, though quitting remains far more beneficial to overall health. 
Lack of Sleep: Too little or too much sleep can affect hormones that regulate appetite and cravings. 
Stress: Stress may encourage consumption of high-calorie comfort foods. 
Microbiome: The composition of gut bacteria may influence weight gain and weight-loss difficulty. 

Having one or more risk factors does not guarantee obesity. Most risk factors can be addressed through healthy lifestyle changes, medications, or medical procedures.

## Complications 

People with obesity are more likely to develop serious health conditions, including: 

### Heart Disease and Stroke 

Obesity increases the likelihood of high blood pressure and unhealthy cholesterol levels. Both are major risk factors for heart disease and stroke. 

### Type 2 Diabetes 

Obesity can interfere with insulin function, increasing the risk of insulin resistance and diabetes. 

### Certain Cancers 

Obesity may increase the risk of cancers involving the Uterus, Cervix, Endometrium, Ovary, Breast, Colon, Rectum, Esophagus, Liver, Gallbladder, Pancreas, Kidney, and Prostate. 

### Digestive Conditions

Obesity increases the likelihood of Heartburn, Gallbladder disease, and Liver disorders. 

### Sleep Apnea 

People with obesity are at increased risk of sleep apnea, a potentially serious condition in which breathing repeatedly stops and starts during sleep. 

### Osteoarthritis 

Extra body weight increases stress on joints and promotes inflammation, contributing to osteoarthritis. 

### Steatotic Liver Disease 

Excess fat accumulation in the liver can lead to liver disease and, in severe cases, liver cirrhosis. 

### Severe COVID-19 Symptoms 

Obesity increases the likelihood of severe illness from COVID-19, potentially requiring intensive care or mechanical ventilation. 

## Quality of Life 

Obesity can significantly reduce overall quality of life. It may limit participation in physical activities and contribute to social challenges or discrimination. 

Common quality-of-life impacts include: 
- Depression 
- Disability 
- Shame and guilt
- Social isolation 
- Lower work achievement
